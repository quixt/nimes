Pont du Gard

Aqueducts moved water through gravity alone, along a slight overall downward gradient within conduits of stone, brick, concrete or lead; the steeper the gradient, the faster the flow. Most conduits were buried beneath the ground and followed the contours of the terrain; obstructing peaks were circumvented or, less often, tunneled through. Where valleys or lowlands intervened, the conduit was carried on bridgework, or its contents fed into high-pressure lead, ceramic, or stone pipes and siphoned across.

The Pont du Gard is a Roman aqueduct that was built in the 1st century AD. It is the tallest and best-preserved Roman aqueduct in the world. The Pont du Gard is 50m high and 9km long. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The Pont du Gard was built to bring water from the springs of the Eure-et-Loir region to the Roman city of Nimes. The aqueduct was in use for over 400 years. It is an engineering marvel and a testimony to the genius of the Roman engineers. The Pont du Gard is one of the most popular tourist attractions in France. Every year, millions of people visit the Pont du Gard to admire its beauty and to learn about its history. The Pont du Gard is a symbol of the Roman empire and its engineering prowess. It is also a reminder of the importance of water in the daily life of the people of Nimes.
